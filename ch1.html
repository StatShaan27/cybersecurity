Introduction to Cybersecurity: Key Concepts

  • Prepare like for a storm: gather tools, secure our environment, and patch vulnerabilities quickly to minimize damage.
  • Cybersecurity means ensuring confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information by protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or exploitation.
  • Security improves confidentiality, e.g., using complex passwords to prevent unauthorized access.
  • Security teams protect against:
    • External threats — outsiders trying to access private info, networks, or devices.
    • Internal threats — current or former employees, vendors, or trusted partners, which can be accidental (e.g., clicking a malicious link) or intentional (e.g., unauthorized data access).
  • Security professionals mitigate or reduce the impact of threats.
  • Security teams ensure regulatory compliance to meet laws and guidelines, helping avoid fines and uphold ethical obligations.
  • Security supports business continuity, allowing work to continue even after incidents like data breaches.
  • Being security conscious reduces expenses related to risk recovery, downtime, and potential fines.
  • Maintaining security preserves brand trust, which affects customer confidence and business revenue.
  • Common job roles include:
    • Security analyst or specialist
    • Cybersecurity analyst or specialist
    • Security operation center (SOC) analyst
    • Information security analyst

Each activity completed in this program builds our knowledge and brings we closer to a cybersecurity career.

Responsibilities of an Entry-Level Cybersecurity Analyst

  • Security analysts monitor and protect information and systems.
  • Protect computer and network systems by monitoring internal networks and responding quickly to detected threats.
  • Participate in exercises like penetration testing or ethical hacking to find vulnerabilities and suggest improvements.
  • Work proactively to prevent threats by collaborating with IT teams to install prevention software identifying risks and vulnerabilities.
  • Support software and hardware development teams to ensure product security and data protection compliance.
  • Conduct periodic security audits reviewing security records, activities, and ensuring confidential information is properly protected.

Entry-level security analysts play a vital role in protecting organizations of all sizes and help keep people safe by performing these day-to-day tasks.

Common Cybersecurity Terminology

Cybersecurity is the practice of ensuring confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information by protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or criminal exploitation.

Key Terms and Concepts

  • Security Analyst / Cybersecurity Analyst: Monitor networks for breaches, develop security strategies, research IT security trends, and work to prevent incidents.
  • Compliance: Adhering to internal standards and external regulations to avoid fines and security breaches.
  • Security Frameworks: Guidelines used to build plans that mitigate risks and threats to data and privacy.
  • Security Controls: Safeguards designed to reduce specific security risks, used with frameworks to build a strong security posture.
  • Security Posture: An organization’s ability to manage defense of critical assets and respond to changes; a strong posture loours organizational risk.
  • Threat Actor: Any person or group posing a security risk to computers, applications, networks, or data.
  • Internal Threat: Risks originating from current or former employees, external vendors, or trusted partners; can be accidental (e.g., clicking a malicious link) or intentional (e.g., unauthorized data access).
  • Network Security: Protecting an organization’s network infrastructure—including data, systems, and devices—from unauthorized access.
  • Cloud Security: Ensuring proper configuration and controlled access to assets stored in cloud data centers accessed via the internet; a growing subfield focused on protecting cloud data, applications, and infrastructure.
  • Programming: Creating instructions for computers to execute tasks such as automating repetitive tasks, reviewing web traffic, and alerting on suspicious activity.

Past Cybersecurity Attacks

  • Overview:
    • The security industry is constantly evolving, but many present-day attacks are adaptations of earlier methods.
    • Understanding past attacks helps security analysts investigate and handle incidents effectively.
    • A computer virus is malicious code designed to disrupt computer operations and damage data/software.
    • Viruses attach to programs or documents and spread across computers in a network.
    • Today, viruses are often called malware, which broadly means software designed to harm devices or networks.
    • Two early malware examples are the Brain virus and the Morris worm.
  • The Brain Virus (1986):
    • Created by the Alvi brothers to track illegal medical software copies and prevent piracy.
    • Instead of just tracking, it infected computers using pirated software.
    • Infection spread through any disk inserted into an infected computer, rapidly spreading globally.
    • Although it did not aim to destroy data or hardware, it slowed productivity and impacted business operations.
    • This virus emphasized the need for security plans to maintain data safety and productivity.
  • The Morris Worm (1988):
    • Developed by Robert Morris to assess the size of the internet by installing itself on connected computers.
    • The worm failed to track compromised computers and kept reinstalling itself, causing crashes due to memory exhaustion.
    • About 6,000 computers (10% of the internet then) were affected.
    • The attack caused millions in damages from business disruption and removal efforts.
    • Resulted in the creation of Computer Emergency Response Teams (CERTs®), which respond to security incidents.
    • CERTs have since expanded responsibilities in the security industry.
  • Summary:
    • Early attacks like these shaped current cybersecurity strategies.
    • Later topics in this program will cover the evolution of attacks in the digital age and hands-on detection and response skills.

Attacks in the Digital Age

  • Internet Expansion and Malware Spread:
    • With reliable high-speed internet, the number of connected computers increased dramatically.
    • Malware no longer needed physical disks to spread; it could propagate through the internet.
  • Notable Internet-Based Attacks:
    • LoveLetter Attack (2000):
      • Created by Onel De Guzman to steal internet login credentials.
      • Spread rapidly by exploiting users' lack of suspicion toward unsolicited emails.
      • Emails had subject line "I Love we" with an attachment "Love Letter For we."
      • Opening the attachment caused the malware to scan the user’s address book and send itself to all contacts.
      • Installed a program to collect user info and passwords.
      • Infected 45 million computers globally, causing over $10 billion in damages.
      • First notable example of social engineering.
    • Social Engineering:
      • A manipulation technique exploiting human error to gain private info, access, or valuables.
      • After LoveLetter, attackers increasingly used social engineering.
      • Rise of social media apps increased data accessibility and user vulnerability.
      • Many prioritize convenience over privacy, leading to increased security risks.
    • Role of Security Professionals:
      • Identify and manage inappropriate technology use that risks the organization.
      • Conduct and participate in regular internal trainings to raise awareness.
      • Employees commonly receive training to recognize social engineering attacks, especially phishing emails.
      • Phishing uses digital communications to trick individuals into revealing sensitive data or deploying malware.
    • Equifax Breach (2017):
      • Attackers infiltrated credit reporting agency Equifax.
      • Stole over 143 million customer records, affecting ~40% of Americans.
      • Compromised data included social security numbers, birth dates, driver’s license numbers, home addresses, credit card numbers.
      • Breach resulted from multiple unpatched vulnerabilities ignored by Equifax.
      • Equifax settled with the U.S. government, paying over $575 million for complaints and fines.
      • This major settlement highlighted the financial consequences of breaches and emphasized preventative measures.
  • Summary and Future Outlook:
    • These well-known incidents shaped the security industry.
    • Knowledge of malware and social engineering is valuable for communication about security risks, including job interviews.
    • Security professionals must continuously adapt to new threat tactics and techniques.
    • Recognizing trends and attack patterns can help identify potential breaches and reduce damage.
    • Understanding how security impacts people’s lives reminds us of the importance of this work.

Common Attacks and Their Effectiveness

Previously, we learned about past and present attacks that helped shape the cybersecurity industry, including the LoveLetter attack (ILOVEwe virus) and the Morris worm. The establishment of response teams, now called computer security incident response teams (CSIRTs), was one outcome. In this reading, we will learn about common attack methods. Familiarity with these and evolving tactics will help we protect organizations and people.

  • Phishing: The use of digital communications to trick people into revealing sensitive data or deploying malicious software.
    • Business Email Compromise (BEC): Threat actor sends an email seeming to come from a known source, requesting information to gain financial advantage.
    • Spear phishing: Targeted malicious email attack on specific user(s) appearing from a trusted source.
    • Whaling: Spear phishing targeting company executives to access sensitive data.
    • Vishing: Exploiting electronic voice communication to obtain sensitive information or impersonate a trusted source.
    • Smishing: Using text messages to trick users into revealing sensitive info or impersonating trusted sources.
  • Malware: Software designed to harm devices or networks, primarily to obtain money or intelligence advantage.
    • Viruses: Malicious code that requires user action to spread via infected attachments or downloads; damages data and software.
    • Worms: Self-replicating malware that spreads independently across networked devices.
    • Ransomware: Encrypts organizational data and demands payment for restoring access.
    • Spyware: Gathers and sells information without consent, accessing private emails, texts, recordings, and locations.
  • Social Engineering: Manipulation exploiting human error and trust to gain private information or access.
    • Social media phishing: Collecting target information from social media to initiate attacks.
    • Watering hole attack: Compromising a website frequently visited by targeted users.
    • USB baiting: Leaving malware-infected USB sticks for employees to unknowingly infect a network.
    • Physical social engineering: Impersonating employees, customers, or vendors to gain unauthorized physical access.
  • Social Engineering Principles — Why Attacks Are Effective:
    • Authority: Impersonation of individuals with poour, exploiting conditioned respect.
    • Intimidation: Using bullying tactics to coerce victims.
    • Consensus/Social proof: Pretending legitimacy by claiming others have complied.
    • Scarcity: Suggesting limited availability to pressure decisions.
    • Familiarity: Establishing fake emotional connections to exploit targets.
    • Trust: Building emotional relationships over time to gain personal information.
    • Urgency: Pressuring quick responses without questioning.
  • Key Takeaways:
    • This reading introduced common attacks and their impacts, especially social engineering’s success.
    • we will have more opportunities throughout this program to deepen our knowledge of identifying and defending against cybersecurity attacks.

Introduction to the Eight CISSP Security Domains

Understanding core security concepts organized into security domains supports our growth as a security professional. CISSP defines eight interrelated domains that cover the work security professionals do. Gaps in any domain can impact an entire organization. Knowing these domains can help we identify career paths and our role within an organization.

Part 1: First Four Domains

  1. Security and Risk Management
    Focuses on defining security goals and objectives, risk mitigation, compliance, business continuity, and law. For example, updating company policies for federal compliance like HIPAA.
  2. Asset Security
    Securing digital and physical assets including storage, maintenance, retention, and destruction of data. Tasks include proper disposal of old equipment containing confidential info.
  3. Security Architecture and Engineering
    Optimizing data security through effective tools, systems, and processes. Example task: configuring firewalls to monitor and filter network traffic to prevent attacks.
  4. Communication and Network Security
    Managing and securing physical networks and wireless communications. For instance, creating network policies to prevent users connecting to unsecured wireless hotspots.

Security is a team effort. As an entry-level analyst, we’ll develop skills to mitigate risks and keep people and data safe. we don’t need to be an expert in all domains, but a basic understanding is essential.

Part 2: Last Four Domains

  1. Identity and Access Management
    Ensuring users follow policies controlling physical assets (e.g., office access) and logical assets (networks, applications). Example: setting up employees’ keycard access.
  2. Security Assessment and Testing
    Conducting security control testing, audits, and analyzing data to monitor risks and vulnerabilities. For example, auditing user permissions to prevent unauthorized access to payroll info.
  3. Security Operations
    Conducting investigations and implementing preventative measures. Example: responding to alerts about unknown devices on the internal network per organizational policies.
  4. Software Development Security
    Using secure coding practices to create secure applications and services. Collaborating with development teams to ensure security in software life cycle, e.g., advising on password policies.

Determine the Type of Attack

Building on our knowledge of the eight CISSP security domains, this reading explores additional attack methods and the types of risks they pose. Understanding which domain an attack falls under helps in managing and mitigating security risks effectively.

Attack Types and Their CISSP Domains

  • Password Attack
    Attempts to access password-secured systems or data. Common forms include:
    - Brute force
    - Rainbow table
    Domain: Communication and Network Security
  • Social Engineering Attack
    Manipulates human error to gain access or information. Common types include:
    - Phishing
    - Smishing
    - Vishing
    - Spear phishing
    - Whaling
    - Social media phishing
    - Business Email Compromise (BEC)
    - Watering hole attack
    - USB baiting
    - Physical social engineering
    Domain: Security and Risk Management
  • Physical Attack
    Security incidents affecting physical environments as well as digital ones. Examples:
    - Malicious USB cable
    - Malicious flash drive
    - Card cloning and skimming
    Domain: Asset Security
  • Adversarial Artificial Intelligence
    Uses AI/ML manipulation to conduct more efficient attacks.
    Domains: Communication and Network Security, Identity and Access Management
  • Supply-Chain Attack
    Targets vulnerabilities in systems, hardware, or software through third-party processes, affecting multiple organizations.
    Domains: Security and Risk Management, Security Architecture and Engineering, Security Operations
  • Cryptographic Attack
    Targets secure communications between sender and recipient. Examples:
    - Birthday attack
    - Collision attack
    - Downgrade attack
    Domain: Communication and Network Security

Key Takeaways

The CISSP security domains provide a framework to understand, prepare for, and defend against various data breaches. Attacks may span multiple domains and vary in complexity. This overview covers only some common attack types that will be studied further throughout the program.

Understand Attackers

Previously, we were introduced to the concept of threat actors. A threat actor is any person or group who presents a security risk. In this reading, we’ll learn about different types of threat actors, their motivations, intentions, and how they’ve influenced the security industry.

Threat Actor Types

  • Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)
    APTs have significant expertise to access an organization's network without authorization. They research their targets in advance and can remain undetected for long periods. Their motivations include:
    - Damaging critical infrastructure like poour grids and natural resources
    - Gaining access to intellectual property such as trade secrets or patents
  • Insider Threats
    These actors abuse authorized access to obtain data that may harm an organization. Their motivations include:
    - Sabotage
    - Corruption
    - Espionage
    - Unauthorized data access or leaks
  • Hacktivists
    Driven by political agendas, they use digital technology to achieve goals such as:
    - Demonstrations
    - Propaganda
    - Social change campaigns
    - Fame

Hacker Types

A hacker is any person who uses computers to gain access to systems, networks, or data. They range from beginners to advanced professionals and have various motivations.

  • Authorized Hackers (Ethical Hackers)
    Follow codes of ethics and laws to conduct risk evaluations. Motivated to protect people and organizations from malicious actors.
  • Semi-Authorized Hackers (Researchers)
    Search for vulnerabilities but do not exploit them.
  • Unauthorized Hackers (Unethical Hackers)
    Malicious actors who break laws to steal and sell confidential data for financial gain.

Other hacker types include:

  • New and unskilled threat actors who seek to learn, seek revenge, or exploit security weaknesses using existing tools and malware.
  • Contract hackers who may be ethical or unethical depending on their tasks.
  • Vigilante hackers aiming to protect the world from unethical hackers.

Key Takeaways

Threat actors are defined by malicious intent, while hackers are defined by their technical skills and motivations. Understanding these aspects helps we prepare to protect our organization and its people from malicious attacks.

Introduction to Security Frameworks and Controls

Imagine you're working as a security analyst and receive multiple alerts about suspicious activity on the network. You realize that you'll need to implement additional security measures to prevent these alerts from becoming serious incidents. But where do you start?

As an analyst, you'll begin by identifying your organization's critical assets and risks. Then, you'll implement the necessary frameworks and controls to manage them effectively.

What Are Security Frameworks?

Security frameworks are structured guidelines that help mitigate risks and threats to data and privacy. They support the implementation of a security lifecycle—a continuously evolving set of standards and policies that manage risk, follow guidelines, and ensure regulatory compliance.

The purposes of security frameworks include:

  • Protecting personally identifiable information (PII)
  • Securing financial information
  • Identifying security weaknesses
  • Managing organizational risks
  • Aligning security with business goals

Four Core Components of Security Frameworks

  • Identifying and Documenting Security Goals
    Example: Aligning with GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) to ensure user data privacy.
  • Setting Guidelines to Achieve Goals
    Example: Creating policies for handling data requests in compliance with GDPR.
  • Implementing Strong Security Processes
    Example: Developing procedures to verify and process user data update or deletion requests.
  • Monitoring and Communicating Results
    Example: Tracking and reporting potential GDPR-related security issues.

Security Controls

Security controls are specific safeguards designed to reduce particular risks. For example, requiring all employees to complete privacy training helps prevent data breaches. As an analyst, you may use software to assign and track training completion automatically.

Key Takeaways

Security frameworks and controls are essential for managing risks in all types of organizations. Understanding how they work allows analysts to collaborate effectively, support organizational goals, and protect data and users.

Secure Design

The CIA Triad

The CIA triad is a foundational model in cybersecurity. It represents three essential principles that guide security policies and system setup:

  • Confidentiality: Only authorized users should have access to specific assets or data. Implementing strict access controls ensures that confidential data stays secure.
  • Integrity: Data must be accurate, authentic, and reliable. Security professionals often use encryption to protect data from unauthorized changes.
  • Availability: Data must be accessible to those with proper authorization. Ensuring system uptime and avoiding disruptions are key goals.

Understanding Assets

An asset is any item perceived to have value to an organization. Its value is often determined by its associated cost or impact. For example:

  • An application storing sensitive data like social security numbers or bank accounts is a high-value asset and requires stringent security.
  • A website sharing public news content is a low-risk asset and may not need the same level of protection.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF)

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework is a voluntary guideline developed by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology. It includes standards and best practices for managing cybersecurity risk. Organizations use it to:

  • Establish a baseline for security practices
  • Manage short-term and long-term risks
  • Align controls with organizational goals and threats

Security teams often consider the motives of threat actors, such as disgruntled employees who may already have access to sensitive information. To mitigate this risk, professionals apply the principle of availability and enforce strict access guidelines—ensuring individuals only access what they need for their role.

Global Threats & Diverse Teams

Threat actors originate from across the globe. A diverse workforce helps bring varied perspectives, which enhances an organization’s ability to detect and mitigate attacks.

Key Takeaway

The CIA Triad and NIST CSF are foundational tools used to secure systems, data, and users. Understanding these principles is essential in both job interviews and real-world scenarios.

Controls, Frameworks, and Compliance

Previously, you were introduced to security frameworks and their role in the security lifecycle. This section expands on how security frameworks, controls, and compliance regulations work together to manage organizational security and reduce risk.

Relationship Between Controls, Frameworks, and Compliance

The CIA triad—confidentiality, integrity, and availability—is a foundational model used to guide security decisions and establish appropriate controls that mitigate threats, risks, and vulnerabilities.

  • Controls: Safeguards designed to reduce specific security risks.
  • Frameworks: Structured guidelines to implement and monitor security practices effectively.
  • Compliance: Adherence to internal standards and external regulations.

Security frameworks typically consist of:

  • Identifying and documenting security goals
  • Setting guidelines to achieve those goals
  • Implementing strong security processes
  • Monitoring and communicating results

Common Frameworks, Controls, and Compliance Standards

  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) and Risk Management Framework (RMF): U.S.-based voluntary standards to manage cybersecurity risk.
  • FERC-NERC: Regulatory body for power grid security; includes Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards.
  • FedRAMP®: Standardizes cloud service security assessment and monitoring for U.S. government use.
  • Center for Internet Security (CIS®): Provides best-practice security controls to safeguard systems and networks.
  • General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR): E.U. law protecting personal data and privacy. Organizations must notify affected individuals within 72 hours of a data breach.
  • PCI DSS: International standard ensuring secure handling of credit card information.
  • HIPAA: U.S. law protecting patients’ health information, supported by three main rules—Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification—and assisted by the HITRUST® framework.
  • ISO: International Organization for Standardization sets cross-border standards in technology, planning, and process management.
  • SOC 1 & SOC 2: AICPA standards assessing user access, data safety, and organizational risk across employee and vendor roles.
  • Executive Order 14028: U.S. directive aimed at improving national cybersecurity, particularly in federal agencies and critical infrastructure.

Pro Tip: Regulations evolve frequently. Consider researching additional regulations such as the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act and the Sarbanes-Oxley Act to stay current.

Key Takeaways

Controls, frameworks, and compliance form the backbone of an organization's security posture. As a security analyst, staying current with frameworks and regulatory changes is essential to protecting both organizations and the people they serve.

Ethics in Cybersecurity

Ethics in cybersecurity guide professionals to make the right decisions when facing security incidents, even when the choices are not clear-cut. As a security analyst, maintaining integrity, confidentiality, and adherence to policies is crucial, regardless of personal relationships or temptations.

Ethical Responsibilities of Security Professionals

Security professionals often have privileged access to sensitive data and systems. This access comes with the ethical duty to:

  • Remain unbiased and objective when investigating incidents.
  • Protect confidentiality and avoid misuse of sensitive information.
  • Follow organizational policies and protocols without favoritism.
  • Avoid abusing access privileges, such as self-granting raises or unauthorized data access.

Key Ethical Principles in Cybersecurity

Confidentiality

Confidentiality means safeguarding proprietary and private information, such as Personally Identifiable Information (PII). Security professionals must ensure this data is not disclosed inappropriately. For example, granting system access to coworkers outside of official channels is unethical and may result in severe consequences.

Privacy Protections

Privacy involves protecting personal information from unauthorized use. Security staff must adhere strictly to company policies regarding data access and sharing. Even when pressured, such as receiving after-hours requests for employee information, the correct response is to follow documented procedures, not circumvent them.

Laws and Compliance

Legal obligations are a fundamental part of cybersecurity ethics. Negligence or failure to protect sensitive data can lead to violations of compliance regulations and legal penalties. For example, leaving confidential patient files unattended is both unethical and illegal, risking data loss and reputational damage.

Why Ethics Matter in Cybersecurity

As technologies evolve and threats become more sophisticated, ethical decision-making ensures security professionals respond appropriately and protect organizational and individual interests. A strong ethical foundation helps maintain trust, uphold security standards, and navigate complex situations with integrity.

Ethical Concepts That Guide Cybersecurity Decisions

Building on the foundation of security ethics, cybersecurity professionals must use ethical principles to make informed and responsible decisions. Ethics help ensure that actions taken to protect organizations and people are legal, fair, and effective in the face of evolving threats.

Ethical Concerns and Laws Around Counterattacks

United States Standpoint

In the U.S., launching counterattacks against threat actors is illegal under laws such as the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986) and the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act (2015). Only defensive actions are legally permitted. Counterattacks are viewed as vigilantism—unauthorized attempts to enforce the law—which can escalate conflicts, cause additional harm, and have severe consequences, especially if the attacker is a state-sponsored hacktivist. Only approved federal or military personnel can conduct counterattacks.

International Standpoint

The International Court of Justice (ICJ) permits counterattacks only under strict conditions:

  • The counterattack affects only the original attacker.
  • It serves as a direct communication demanding the attacker stop.
  • It does not escalate the conflict.
  • Its effects can be reversed.

Because these conditions are difficult to verify, organizations generally avoid counterattacks due to legal ambiguity and the risk of worsening the situation.

Core Ethical Principles and Methodologies

Since counterattacks are mostly disallowed or illegal, cybersecurity relies on frameworks such as the CIA triad to address ethical obligations. These include:

Confidentiality

Ensuring that only authorized users access sensitive data. Ethically, this means respecting privacy and safeguarding private assets.

Privacy Protection

Protecting personal information—including Personally Identifiable Information (PII) and Sensitive PII (SPII)—from unauthorized use. Security professionals have an ethical duty to secure such data, identify vulnerabilities, manage risks, and align security efforts with organizational goals.

Laws and Legal Compliance

Cybersecurity professionals must:

  • Remain unbiased and honest in their work.
  • Respect and uphold laws applicable to data and security.
  • Be transparent, evidence-based, and just in decision-making.
  • Continuously update skills and knowledge to better serve the cyber ecosystem.

For example, compliance with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) requires protecting patients’ health information and notifying them if a breach occurs.

Key Takeaways

Understanding and applying ethical concepts and laws are essential for cybersecurity professionals to navigate threats responsibly, protect their organizations, and maintain trust in their work.

Key Cybersecurity Terms and Concepts

Understanding fundamental cybersecurity terminology is essential for grasping how organizations protect their data, systems, and people. Below are important concepts you will encounter regularly in cybersecurity practice and study.

  • Asset: Any item perceived as valuable to an organization, such as data, hardware, software, or intellectual property.
  • Availability: Ensuring that data and systems are accessible to authorized users when needed.
  • Compliance: The process of adhering to both internal policies and external regulations relevant to security and privacy.
  • Confidentiality: Restricting access to specific assets or data only to authorized users, maintaining privacy and secrecy.
  • Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability (CIA) Triad: A foundational security model used to evaluate risks and guide policies that protect data confidentiality, data integrity, and system availability.
  • Hacktivist: An individual who uses hacking techniques to promote political or social goals, often through disruptive or unauthorized cyber actions.
  • Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA): A U.S. federal law that protects patients’ health information and mandates strict controls over its use and disclosure.
  • Integrity: The assurance that data is accurate, authentic, and reliable without unauthorized modification.
  • National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework (CSF): A voluntary framework consisting of best practices, standards, and guidelines to help organizations manage cybersecurity risks.
  • Privacy Protection: The act of safeguarding personal information from unauthorized access or misuse.
  • Protected Health Information (PHI): Information related to an individual's physical or mental health, past, present, or future, protected under laws like HIPAA.
  • Security Architecture: A comprehensive design of security components, tools, and processes aimed at protecting an organization from threats and vulnerabilities.
  • Security Controls: Specific safeguards or countermeasures implemented to reduce security risks.
  • Security Ethics: Principles and guidelines that help cybersecurity professionals make ethical decisions, maintaining integrity, confidentiality, and trust.
  • Security Frameworks: Structured guidelines and plans used to design, implement, and monitor security practices to reduce risk.
  • Security Governance: Organizational practices and policies that define, support, and direct security initiatives and responsibilities.
  • Sensitive Personally Identifiable Information (SPII): A subset of PII that requires stricter protection due to its potential to cause harm if exposed, such as social security numbers or financial details.

Summary: These terms form the core language and concepts of cybersecurity, helping professionals understand how to protect assets, comply with laws, and ethically manage security risks.

Common Cybersecurity Tools

Cybersecurity professionals use various tools to detect, assess, and mitigate threats. These tools streamline processes, analyze large volumes of data, and help protect systems against vulnerabilities. At the heart of these tools are logs—records of events like user logins or system access—that provide the data for analysis.

Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) Tools

SIEM tools collect and analyze log data from various systems in real time, allowing security analysts to monitor for critical activities and detect potential breaches. They reduce data overload by generating alerts based on specific risks or threats.

Examples of SIEM Tools

  • Splunk Enterprise: A self-hosted data analysis platform used to retain, search, and analyze organizational log data.
  • Google Chronicle: A cloud-native SIEM tool that supports fast feature delivery and enables scalable search and analysis of security data.

SIEM tools allow analysts to analyze patterns, conduct incident investigations, and proactively hunt for threats. Though implementations may vary across organizations, the core goal is to minimize risk through informed data monitoring and response.

Playbooks

Playbooks are operational manuals detailing how to respond to incidents and conduct procedures like security reviews or access management. These documents help ensure consistency in how incidents are handled before, during, and after they occur. Each organization may tailor their playbooks to fit specific needs and workflows.

Network Protocol Analyzers (Packet Sniffers)

These tools capture and analyze network traffic to identify suspicious or unauthorized activity. They are essential for assessing data movement within a network.

Examples of Network Protocol Analyzers

  • tcpdump: A command-line packet analyzer for capturing and inspecting network traffic.
  • Wireshark: A graphical tool used to analyze network traffic and troubleshoot security issues.

Entry-level analysts are not expected to master these tools immediately, but continued hands-on experience builds proficiency and strengthens risk mitigation capabilities over time.

Tools for Protecting Business Operations

Security analysts use a variety of tools to mitigate risks and protect organizational operations. While toolkits may vary by organization, familiarity with standard tools and the ability to learn similar ones are essential for any analyst. These tools help with log analysis, threat detection, incident response, and forensic investigations.

Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) Tools

SIEM tools collect and analyze log data to monitor critical organizational activities. They generate alerts for threats and display data in interactive dashboards for easier review. Depending on the volume of data, SIEM tools dramatically reduce the time and effort required to detect potential risks.

SIEM dashboards vary across tools, displaying accessible information in categories to assist users in their analysis. These tools may be hosted on-premise or in the cloud. Cloud-hosted options are generally easier to deploy and maintain, making them preferable for teams with less experience.

Network Protocol Analyzers (Packet Sniffers)

Network protocol analyzers, or packet sniffers, capture and analyze data traffic within a network. These tools record data encountered by computers on a network, allowing analysts to assess traffic patterns and detect irregularities. Common tools in this category will be explored in practice sessions later in training.

Playbooks

Playbooks are manuals detailing how to handle specific operational or security-related tasks. They help ensure analysts follow consistent protocols across different scenarios. Every organization has unique playbooks, but they all serve the purpose of guiding structured and reliable responses to incidents.

Example Use Case

Consider a forensic investigation following a breach at a medical practice. As an analyst, your role involves collecting evidence and supporting an insurance claim. Playbooks would guide each step of this investigation to ensure accuracy and protocol adherence.

Chain of Custody Playbook

This playbook documents the possession and handling of digital evidence throughout an incident lifecycle. It records who had access to what evidence, when, and why. Maintaining a secure chain of custody ensures that the evidence is credible and admissible in legal or regulatory reviews.

Protecting and Preserving Evidence Playbook

This playbook outlines how to handle fragile digital evidence, especially volatile data that can be lost when a device powers off. Analysts must preserve this data using proper procedures, such as copying evidence before analysis. Failure to follow these steps can result in compromised evidence that is no longer valid.

Key Takeaways

Security analysts must understand and effectively use tools like SIEM platforms, packet sniffers, and playbooks to protect operations and respond to incidents. While only two forensic playbooks were covered—chain of custody and protecting/preserving evidence—they form a crucial foundation for investigations. Further exploration of forensic practices and tools is recommended for those interested in this career path.

Introduction to Linux, SQL, and Python

In addition to tools like SIEMs, playbooks, and packet sniffers, security analysts rely on programming languages and operating systems to automate tasks, manage data, and enhance overall efficiency. Understanding tools like Linux, SQL, and Python is essential for performing technical security tasks with accuracy and speed.

Linux

Linux is an open-source operating system that uses a command-line interface for user interaction. Unlike graphical operating systems like Windows or MacOS, Linux relies on text-based commands to execute operations. While not a programming language, Linux enables analysts to examine system logs and interact directly with the system’s architecture.

For example, entry-level analysts might use Linux commands to review error logs during an investigation of unusual network activity. Mastery of the Linux command line is a key skill for analyzing logs and managing system-level tasks efficiently.

SQL (Structured Query Language)

SQL is used to interact with and extract information from databases. These databases can hold millions of data points, and SQL allows analysts to filter, query, and retrieve specific entries relevant to a security investigation. By writing efficient SQL queries, analysts can locate crucial evidence or indicators of compromise within massive datasets.

Python

Python is a powerful, high-level programming language used to automate repetitive and time-consuming security tasks. It allows for rapid development of scripts and tools that help detect anomalies, process logs, or even simulate attacks for testing purposes. Analysts rely on Python for its readability, versatility, and robust library support.

Key Takeaways

As a future security analyst, it’s important to understand that each organization’s toolkit may differ. Familiarity with Linux, SQL, and Python demonstrates your ability to learn new tools and adapt to the specific needs of an organization. These foundational technologies are widely used across the industry and will be explored further in the certificate program through hands-on practice.

Use Tools to Protect Business Operations

Cybersecurity professionals use a wide range of tools, programming languages, and operating systems to protect systems and data. As an entry-level analyst, understanding the purpose and function of these tools will help you contribute effectively to your organization's security operations.

Programming

Programming allows analysts to automate repetitive tasks, reducing human error and increasing efficiency. Languages like Python are used to create scripts that perform automated checks, process data, and manage system behavior. SQL (Structured Query Language) is used to extract specific data points from large databases, allowing analysts to identify trends, filter information, and support investigations.

Operating Systems

Operating systems (OS) act as intermediaries between users and computer hardware. Common OS options include Linux, macOS, and Windows. Linux, in particular, is favored in cybersecurity due to its open-source nature and command-line interface, which enables advanced system control and log analysis. You'll explore Linux in greater detail later in the program, including its kernel and the GNU components that support it.

Web Vulnerabilities

Web vulnerabilities are flaws in web applications that can be exploited by threat actors to gain unauthorized access, steal data, or deploy malware. Staying updated with common vulnerabilities is critical. The OWASP Top 10 is a key resource for understanding current web application security risks.

Antivirus Software

Antivirus, or anti-malware software, detects, prevents, and removes malicious software. These tools scan system memory for patterns associated with malware and help protect against infections that can compromise system integrity.

Intrusion Detection System (IDS)

An IDS monitors network traffic for suspicious behavior or unauthorized access attempts. By analyzing packets of data moving through the network, an IDS can alert analysts to potential threats such as intrusions or data theft.

Encryption

Encryption protects the confidentiality of data by converting readable information (plaintext) into an unreadable format (ciphertext). This ensures that even if data is intercepted, it cannot be understood by unauthorized users. Encryption differs from encoding, which uses public algorithms to allow different systems to share information without focusing on security.

Penetration Testing

Penetration testing, or pen testing, is a simulated cyberattack conducted to identify vulnerabilities in systems, applications, or processes. Pen testing evaluates both internal and external threats, helping organizations strengthen their defenses before real attacks occur.

Key Takeaways

In this reading, you explored programming, operating systems, and a variety of tools used by security analysts. Each organization selects the tools that align with its security goals, so developing familiarity with a broad set of tools increases your versatility and value in the field.
