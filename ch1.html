Introduction to Cybersecurity: Key Concepts

  • Prepare like for a storm: gather tools, secure your environment, and patch vulnerabilities quickly to minimize damage.
  • Cybersecurity means ensuring confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information by protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or exploitation.
  • Security improves confidentiality, e.g., using complex passwords to prevent unauthorized access.
  • Security teams protect against:
    • External threats — outsiders trying to access private info, networks, or devices.
    • Internal threats — current or former employees, vendors, or trusted partners, which can be accidental (e.g., clicking a malicious link) or intentional (e.g., unauthorized data access).
  • Security professionals mitigate or reduce the impact of threats.
  • Security teams ensure regulatory compliance to meet laws and guidelines, helping avoid fines and uphold ethical obligations.
  • Security supports business continuity, allowing work to continue even after incidents like data breaches.
  • Being security conscious reduces expenses related to risk recovery, downtime, and potential fines.
  • Maintaining security preserves brand trust, which affects customer confidence and business revenue.
  • Common job roles include:
    • Security analyst or specialist
    • Cybersecurity analyst or specialist
    • Security operation center (SOC) analyst
    • Information security analyst

Each activity completed in this program builds your knowledge and brings you closer to a cybersecurity career.

Responsibilities of an Entry-Level Cybersecurity Analyst

  • Security analysts monitor and protect information and systems.
  • Protect computer and network systems by monitoring internal networks and responding quickly to detected threats.
  • Participate in exercises like penetration testing or ethical hacking to find vulnerabilities and suggest improvements.
  • Work proactively to prevent threats by collaborating with IT teams to install prevention software identifying risks and vulnerabilities.
  • Support software and hardware development teams to ensure product security and data protection compliance.
  • Conduct periodic security audits reviewing security records, activities, and ensuring confidential information is properly protected.

Entry-level security analysts play a vital role in protecting organizations of all sizes and help keep people safe by performing these day-to-day tasks.

Common Cybersecurity Terminology

Cybersecurity is the practice of ensuring confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information by protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or criminal exploitation.

Key Terms and Concepts

  • Security Analyst / Cybersecurity Analyst: Monitor networks for breaches, develop security strategies, research IT security trends, and work to prevent incidents.
  • Compliance: Adhering to internal standards and external regulations to avoid fines and security breaches.
  • Security Frameworks: Guidelines used to build plans that mitigate risks and threats to data and privacy.
  • Security Controls: Safeguards designed to reduce specific security risks, used with frameworks to build a strong security posture.
  • Security Posture: An organization’s ability to manage defense of critical assets and respond to changes; a strong posture lowers organizational risk.
  • Threat Actor: Any person or group posing a security risk to computers, applications, networks, or data.
  • Internal Threat: Risks originating from current or former employees, external vendors, or trusted partners; can be accidental (e.g., clicking a malicious link) or intentional (e.g., unauthorized data access).
  • Network Security: Protecting an organization’s network infrastructure—including data, systems, and devices—from unauthorized access.
  • Cloud Security: Ensuring proper configuration and controlled access to assets stored in cloud data centers accessed via the internet; a growing subfield focused on protecting cloud data, applications, and infrastructure.
  • Programming: Creating instructions for computers to execute tasks such as automating repetitive tasks, reviewing web traffic, and alerting on suspicious activity.

Key Takeaway

Understanding these terms and concepts prepares you for your role as a security analyst and helps identify common threats, risks, and vulnerabilities.

Core Skills for Cybersecurity Professionals

Transferable Skills

  • Communication: Explaining threats, risks, and vulnerabilities to non-technical audiences; interpreting and communicating policies and reporting findings.
  • Collaboration: Working with teams including engineers, forensic investigators, project managers, and ethical hackers to implement security measures.
  • Problem-Solving: Identifying, diagnosing, and solving security problems; understanding threat actors and trends to handle future threats.

Technical Skills

  • Programming: Basic understanding of languages like Python and SQL for automating tasks and identifying errors. No prior experience required; hands-on practice included.
  • Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) Tools: Using SIEM tools to detect and analyze threats, risks, and vulnerabilities; for example, investigating alerts of unauthorized system access.
  • Computer Forensics: Identifying, analyzing, and preserving digital evidence in networks, computers, and devices, similar to forensic scientists in criminal investigations.

Additional Insights

Intellectual curiosity and motivation to continually learn are crucial for success in cybersecurity. Regular learning of programming and security tools is recommended.

As you progress, you will develop the knowledge and skills necessary for a career in security.

The Importance of Cybersecurity

  • Why Security Matters:
    • Security professionals protect physical and digital assets.
    • Organizations and governments need to manage risk.
    • Security ensures business continuity and maintains ethical standing.
  • Impact of Data Breaches:
    • Data breaches affect organizations’ reputations and the people connected to them.
    • Strong security increases user trust, which can lead to financial growth and referrals.
  • Protecting Personal Data:
    • Organizations must secure user, customer, and vendor data to prevent exposure.
    • Personally Identifiable Information (PII) includes full name, DOB, address, phone, email, IP address, etc.
    • Sensitive PII (SPII) includes social security numbers, medical/financial info, biometric data like facial recognition.
    • SPII theft is more damaging than regular PII theft.
  • Threats from Breaches:
    • PII and SPII are prime targets for threat actors.
    • Identity theft is a common consequence, aiming for financial fraud by impersonation.
  • Growing Need for Cybersecurity Professionals:
    • Security analysts are crucial to protect data, products, and people.
    • They ensure confidentiality, integrity, and safe access to information.
    • Demand for security professionals is projected to grow by 30%+ by 2030 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics).
  • Encouragement:
    • Continue learning to contribute to safer and more secure environments for organizations and individuals.

Cybersecurity Glossary

  • Cybersecurity (or security):
    • The practice of ensuring confidentiality, integrity, and availability of information by protecting networks, devices, people, and data from unauthorized access or criminal exploitation.
  • Cloud security:
    • The process of ensuring that assets stored in the cloud are properly configured and access to those assets is limited to authorized users.
  • Internal threat:
    • A current or former employee, external vendor, or trusted partner who poses a security risk.
  • Network security:
    • The practice of keeping an organization's network infrastructure secure from unauthorized access.
  • Personally identifiable information (PII):
    • Any information used to infer an individual’s identity.
  • Security posture:
    • An organization’s ability to manage its defense of critical assets and data and react to change.
  • Sensitive personally identifiable information (SPII):
    • A specific type of PII that falls under stricter handling guidelines.
  • Technical skills:
    • Skills that require knowledge of specific tools, procedures, and policies.
  • Threat:
    • Any circumstance or event that can negatively impact assets.
  • Threat actor:
    • Any person or group who presents a security risk.
  • Transferable skills:
    • Skills from other areas that can apply to different careers.
