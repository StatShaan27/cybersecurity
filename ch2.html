1. Explore the CISSP Security Domains

  • CISSP outlines eight domains that help organizations structure their security efforts and assess vulnerabilities. These domains shape a strong security posture—an organization's ability to defend assets and respond to threats.
  • The first four domains are:
    • Security and Risk Management:
      • Focuses on defining security goals and reducing risk to critical data (like PII).
      • Includes compliance, business continuity, and legal/ethical regulations.
      • Involves setting policies and disaster recovery plans to ensure uninterrupted operations.
    • Asset Security:
      • Ensures secure handling of digital and physical assets, including data storage, retention, and disposal.
      • Protects sensitive data (e.g., PII, SPII) during storage, transfer, and destruction.
      • Emphasizes knowing what data exists and who has access to it.
    • Security Architecture and Engineering:
      • Focuses on implementing effective tools and systems to secure assets.
      • Promotes shared responsibility—everyone plays a role in maintaining security.
      • Encourages quick response to security concerns through user involvement.
    • Communication and Network Security:
      • Manages secure physical and wireless communication channels.
      • Addresses risks from remote work, like insecure Bluetooth or public Wi-Fi.
      • Security teams may restrict unsafe network options to reduce attack surfaces.
  • The remaining four domains include:
    • Identity and Access Management (IAM):
      • Controls who can access systems and data.
      • Key components: identification, authentication, authorization, and accountability.
      • Reduces risk by limiting access to only what users need.
    • Security Assessment and Testing:
      • Involves evaluating security controls, conducting audits, and analyzing data.
      • Helps ensure controls align with goals and improve resilience to threats.
      • Examples include assessments that lead to implementing multi-factor authentication.
    • Security Operations:
      • Focuses on responding to incidents and conducting forensic investigations.
      • Urgency is key when identifying and mitigating active threats.
      • Investigations uncover how breaches occurred and help prevent future attacks.
    • Software Development Security:
      • Integrates secure coding practices throughout the software development lifecycle (SDLC).
      • Security reviews happen during design, development, testing, and deployment phases.
      • Goal: ensure secure applications and protect sensitive data from inception to release.

Understanding these eight CISSP domains helps security professionals build, test, and maintain strong defenses while ensuring ethical practices and organizational continuity.

1.1 CISSP Security Domains: Overview

  • CISSP defines eight security domains that help security teams organize tasks, identify gaps, and establish an organization’s security posture — its ability to defend critical assets and respond to changes.
  • The first four domains focus on foundational elements:
    • Security and Risk Management
      • Establishes security goals and objectives to reduce risks to sensitive data like PII.
      • Includes risk mitigation protocols, legal compliance, and business continuity planning (e.g., disaster recovery).
      • Promotes ethical behavior to reduce fraud, negligence, and abuse.
    • Asset Security
      • Secures digital and physical assets throughout their lifecycle — from storage and transmission to destruction.
      • Policies ensure sensitive data (e.g., PII/SPII) is handled securely, including destruction of physical media (e.g., hard drives).
    • Security Architecture and Engineering
      • Ensures that tools, systems, and processes protect organizational data effectively.
      • Emphasizes shared responsibility — everyone in the organization contributes to reducing risk.
      • Encourages users to report concerns, helping teams respond quickly.
    • Communication and Network Security
      • Secures physical networks and wireless communications — on-site, in the cloud, or remotely.
      • Controls prevent risky behavior, such as using public Wi-Fi or insecure Bluetooth connections.
  • The next four domains focus on access, assessment, operations, and software:
    • Identity and Access Management (IAM)
      • Controls access to assets by verifying and authorizing users.
      • Applies principle of least privilege: users get only the access needed to do their job.
      • Includes: Identification (username/ID), Authentication (password/biometrics), Authorization (role-based access), and Accountability (logging user actions).
    • Security Assessment and Testing
      • Evaluates whether current security controls are effective in managing threats.
      • Involves regular testing (e.g., audits, assessments, pen testing) to enhance security controls like MFA.
    • Security Operations
      • Responds to and investigates security incidents (e.g., breaches) using forensic tools and techniques.
      • Focuses on mitigation, evidence collection, and implementing post-incident improvements.
    • Software Development Security
      • Integrates security into every phase of the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC).
      • Includes secure coding, code reviews, design reviews, and penetration testing to protect software and user data.
  • Understanding these domains equips analysts to better secure systems and contribute effectively to cybersecurity teams.

Each domain plays a vital role in strengthening an organization's cybersecurity infrastructure and helps guide the daily work of analysts in diverse roles.

2. Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

  • Organizations manage a wide range of assets — anything with value, including:
    • Physical items (e.g., office spaces, hardware)
    • Digital data (e.g., customer PII, intellectual property)
  • Threat — Any circumstance or event that can negatively impact an asset.
    • Example: Phishing (a social engineering attack) — tricking users via email to disclose private data like credentials or financial info.
    • Other threats: malware, insider attacks, natural disasters.
  • Risk — The potential that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and harm an asset.
    • Risk = Threat × Vulnerability
    • Example: No backup strategy = high risk of data loss during a ransomware attack.
    • Risk levels:
      • Low risk: Public data (e.g., blog posts, press releases).
      • Medium risk: Internal documents (e.g., financial reports, internal memos).
      • High risk: Protected data (e.g., PII, SPII, or intellectual property).
  • Vulnerability — A weakness that could be exploited by a threat.
    • Examples: Outdated software, weak passwords, exposed sensitive data.
    • People can also be vulnerabilities — through negligence or lack of awareness.
    • Note: Both a threat and a vulnerability must exist for a risk to be present.
  • Mitigation Strategies for Entry-Level Analysts:
    • Educate users to identify threats like phishing emails.
    • Promote strong access controls (e.g., ID cards for physical entry).
    • Encourage reporting of suspicious activity.
    • Regularly audit access to critical assets.
  • Analysts play a key role in reducing risks by identifying vulnerabilities and helping teams respond to threats proactively.

Understanding the relationships between threats, risks, and vulnerabilities helps analysts design better protections for organizational assets and people.

2.1 Key Impacts of Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

  • Security threats like ransomware can severely disrupt operations by:
    • Encrypting data and freezing systems until a ransom is paid.
    • Disrupting business continuity and delaying recovery.
    • Leading to sensitive data exposure on the dark web.
  • The three major impacts on organizations include:
    • 1. Financial Losses
      • Downtime in production and services.
      • Cost of recovery and remediation.
      • Regulatory fines due to non-compliance.
    • 2. Identity Theft
      • Compromise of sensitive PII (e.g., customer, employee, vendor data).
      • Leaked data often sold on the dark web, increasing legal and ethical concerns.
    • 3. Reputational Damage
      • Loss of customer trust and loyalty.
      • Bad publicity and possible long-term brand damage.
      • Potential legal action or penalties.
  • Organizations are urged to implement proactive security protocols to mitigate these impacts, especially in response to threats like ransomware.

Understanding the consequences of threats, risks, and vulnerabilities enables analysts to better safeguard operations and uphold organizational trust and resilience.

3. NIST’s Risk Management Framework (RMF)

  • The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) provides widely adopted frameworks for managing cybersecurity risks. One of the most important is the Risk Management Framework (RMF).
  • There are seven steps in the RMF. While entry-level analysts may not engage in every step, understanding each is essential:
    1. 1. Prepare
      • Initial activities to identify and manage risks before an incident occurs.
      • Involves setting objectives and identifying risk-reducing controls.
    2. 2. Categorize
      • Processes to assess how risks affect the Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability (CIA) of systems and information.
      • Helps align risk priorities with critical organizational assets.
    3. 3. Select
      • Choosing appropriate security controls and maintaining up-to-date documentation (e.g., playbooks).
      • Tailors protections to organizational needs.
    4. 4. Implement
      • Applying the selected security and privacy controls in practice.
      • May include changes to processes like password policies based on observed patterns.
    5. 5. Assess
      • Evaluating whether controls are implemented correctly and functioning as intended.
      • Identifies weaknesses or inefficiencies that could lead to vulnerabilities.
    6. 6. Authorize
      • Accepting and being accountable for the risks that remain.
      • Involves creating reports, plans of action, and aligning security goals with business operations.
    7. 7. Monitor
      • Ongoing surveillance of systems and controls to ensure they continue to meet security objectives.
      • Enables quick response if systems deviate from expected performance or introduce new risks.
  • Although analysts may not directly implement or authorize procedures, it’s crucial they know how to monitor and verify their effectiveness in protecting the organization and its users.

Familiarity with the RMF gives cybersecurity professionals a structured way to mitigate and manage risks across their organization.

3.1 Manage Common Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

  • Understanding the threat landscape is critical for creating policies that prevent and mitigate security issues. Organizations protect both digital and physical assets using strategic frameworks and up-to-date threat intelligence.
  • Risk Management Strategies:
    • Acceptance: Accepting certain risks to maintain business continuity.
    • Avoidance: Developing plans to completely avoid certain risks.
    • Transference: Passing the risk to a third party (e.g., insurance or outsourced security).
    • Mitigation: Reducing the impact of known risks through proactive measures.
  • Common Frameworks:
    • NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)
    • Health Information Trust Alliance (HITRUST)
  • Threats: Events or circumstances that can negatively impact assets.
    • Insider Threats: Authorized individuals abusing access.
    • Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs): Long-term unauthorized access maintained by threat actors.
  • Risks: Anything that could affect Confidentiality, Integrity, or Availability (CIA) of assets.
    • External Risk: Attacks from outside the organization.
    • Internal Risk: Malicious or careless insiders.
    • Legacy Systems: Outdated technologies that remain connected to critical systems.
    • Multiparty Risk: Risks due to third-party vendors and shared resources.
    • Software Compliance: Non-compliance or unpatched systems create vulnerabilities.

    Refer to the OWASP Top 10 and NIST risk resources for updated threat trends.

  • Vulnerabilities: Weaknesses in systems that can be exploited by threats.
    • ProxyLogon: Exploits Microsoft Exchange servers remotely.
    • ZeroLogon: Exploits Microsoft's Netlogon protocol to bypass authentication.
    • Log4Shell: Java-based vulnerability that allows remote code execution.
    • PetitPotam: Exploits NTLM authentication protocols in Windows networks.
    • Security Logging Failures: Poor monitoring allows undetected attacks.
    • Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF): Manipulates server-side applications to gain unauthorized backend access.

    For ongoing updates, visit the NIST National Vulnerability Database and CISA KEV Catalog.

  • Key Takeaway: Security analysts must proactively understand and manage threats, risks, and vulnerabilities. Staying up-to-date with threat intelligence and frameworks ensures stronger defenses and quicker mitigation of cyber threats.
